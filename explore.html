Pegasus3D Vision

One score and several quadrillion nanoseconds ago, our web-father brought forth upon this Internet, a new world-wide web, conceived in hypertext connectivity, and dedicated to the proposition that anyone may freely use or modify it. The simple web that Tim Berners-Lee wove in CERN’s lab now stretches around the globe, encompassing billions of colorful, interactive, interconnected pages offered up by millions of independent sources.

But these pages, they are flat! Sometimes we want to immerse ourselves within three-dimensional virtual worlds, where we can explore, play, and make things happen in a way that feels like a more natural extension to the three dimensional world we inhabit than flipping through virtual pieces of paper. To make this possible, we must design and build an comparable Web3D eco-system of browsers, servers and standards, inspired and informed by existing world-wide web technologies.

Until a good working demonstration of Web3D has been created, we must use these inadequate words on a page to imagine how users would experience this 3-dimensional web.

Scenes and Objects

The 3-D equivalent to a web page is a scene. A scene might be a virtual house, a landscape, a neighborhood or city, a store or marketplace, outer space, a game zone, a simulation, a living organism, and so much more. Every scene defines the location of all static and animated objects within its boundaries, as well as a view of the space that lies beyond its boundaries (such as the scene’s terrain or floor and its sky or bounding walls).

A scene consists of various types of objects:

  • Static Scenery. Static objects establish the scene’s overall look and layout, including the landscape, buildings, flora and fauna, furniture, skyscrape, and any other decorations whose position and behavior never (or rarely) changes. These objects might employ some animation (such as flowing, rippling water or the swaying branches of a tree). Passive objects can be solid (obstacles to or pedestals for other objects) or immersive (other objects can pass through them, but perhaps with some potential effect). They may cast light or shadows, or distort views (like a smoky window or mirror). They may exert a force on other objects (such as gravity).
  • Interactive agents. These objects have more sophisticated, scripted behavior that is triggered by or influences other scene objects. Their animated motion may represent a current state (running, climbing, waving, bowing, fighting, etc.). They may offer buttons or other controls that can be manipulated by users or other objects. They might have wallpaper surfaces that display video streams, images, web pages and more. They may have sensors which will trigger scripted behavior based on what they are near or what is happening around or to them.

    Truly amazing things are possible when the behavior of objects originates somewhere else on the Internet or the real-world. Imagine a mailbox that fully connects to your e-mail service provider. Imagine an accurate clock and calendar, synced to your time zone and appointments. Imagine a phone connected to your Skype account, able to place and receive calls around the world. Imagine a painting on your house’s wall that can play movies from Netflix.

  • Avatars. Each avatar in a scene represents a user that is currently visiting that scene. Avatars are interactive agents whose behavior is directly controlled by the user it represents. The look of each user’s avatar represents an interesting challenge, as user’s will want the flexibility to choose what their avatar looks like (even to the point of using the same avatar across every scene they might visit), but each scene’s owner may want to restrict the look of avatars for functional or community policy reasons. This issue is discussed in greater depth below.
  • Portals. These interactive objects (or specified regions at the outer boundary of the scene) operate like hyperlinks, providing a way to transport a user to another other Web3D scene or to another location within the current scene. The portal might be triggered by proximity or by using its provided controls. Since portals represent a gateway to another scene, it might render some perspective of the scene it references as part of its visual look, typically with a lower level of detail.
  • Sound Sources. A scene can include sounds as well as sights. A scene’s sound source can be ambient to a specific region within the scene, have a specific location in space, or be attached to a specific user wherever they go. A sound source might be music, an audio clip, or live audio streamed across the Internet.

Scene View and Navigation

At any point in time, the user views a scene from the perspective of an imaginary camera which is positioned relative to the location and orientation of their avatar. Generally this is a  2-dimensional or stereoscopic projection of the scene. Various windows, icons and text may be overlaid onto this view based on how the user interacts with the scene.

Users navigate their avatar (and its accompanying camera view) through a scene using standard controls. Such controls move the avatar forward, back, right, left, up or down, or change the avatar’s orientation. Some controls change the camera view that is loosely tied to the avatar’s position and orientation (such as scrolling the camera back behind an avatar’s “eyes”).

Although many controls are standardized, the effect they have varies depending on the scene’s movement rules:

  • Each scene defines its own physics rules for how objects move or collide. Movement behavior will be vary noticeably between land, outer space and underwater scenes, affected by gravity, friction, momentum, etc.
  • Some scenes may choose to restrict or not support certain kinds of movement.
  • The user’s control devices will implement these standard controls in different ways. A mouse/keyboard user will navigate differently from a touch screen or glasses user.
  • Users may customize how their device’s buttons are mapped to the standard navigation controls. Some users may prefer to use the up arrow to move forward, and others may prefer to use the “w” key.

The key idea here is that once a user learns how to navigate any specific 3D scene, they already have a basic idea of how to navigate through any other 3D scene they might visit.

Scene Interaction and Manipulation

Although some scenes will be built primarily for passive consumption (like a book or a movie), the greater value in other scenes comes from all the ways users can interact with the scene and other users in that scene. A user can do so in various ways:

  • Targeted Object. A user targets a specific object using consistent controls (such as touching or clicking it, tab-cycling through interactive objects in-front of the user, etc.). A “use” touch (e.g., left-click) triggers the default action for the object. A “query” touch (e.g., right click) offers a window menu of actions the user can select between.  
  • Object Controls. Some objects may offer touch-sensitive controls on their surface. Each control governs a different aspect of the object’s behavior (for example, a music player might have different controls to play, rewind, change playlist, etc.)
  • Broadcast Action. A user may broadcast some action to all objects within a certain distance of their avatar, such as saying something, issuing a challenge, or swinging a weapon.
  • Proximity. Some objects trigger specific scripted behavior whenever another object or avatar’s position gets near it in a certain direction.

The scene and the object’s script determine how it will respond to any of these triggers. The interaction may be ignored, may yield a change in the object’s behavior (visual or auditory), or the object may display a custom window on the user’s screen either to display additional information or to gather more information about what the user wants. The user interacts with these windows similarly to any web page, with the final action typically removing the window from the screen. The user can sometimes customize the position and size of this window on their screen for future interactions with similar objects. In many cases, the displayed window may actually be a real web page sourced from another site.

In some cases, users may wish to “pin” an object’s window to their screen, which enables them to continue to interact with an object even when they have moved far from that object and are possibly in another completely unrelated scene. This pinned window can be minimized to an small icon on the screen and opened up later as needed. With this simple, but powerful mechanism, users can accumulate their most frequently used resources with them (chat, phone, e-mail, clock, calendar, portal bookmarks, etc.) and take them with them wherever they go. They can also use this mechanism to create hotkeys for common objects actions appropriate to specific scenes or situations. These icons can further be mapped to the user’s device control, such as keyboard keys, mouse buttons, etc.

Some scenes may give authorized users the power to add or delete objects to the scene, reposition or resize them, change their properties or behavioral scripts, and more. Standard controls are defined for a basic core set of object manipulations.

Ownership Controls

Every scene is owned and operated by the server at that scene’s domain name. People entering that scene may be required to be pre-authorized to enter and will typically be asked to abide that site’s rules of behavior.

Each web site that wants to limit access to their 3-d worlds can require users to register, login and interact via encrypted communications.

Similarly, 3-D web site owners can require users pay for access to all or some portion of their content or services.

Avatars

In many cases, users enjoy being able to choose how they appear as an animated 3-D object (or being) within any particular scene. However, specific scenes may have legitimate reasons for wanting to restrict how those avatars might appear to other users within that scene. A scene in outer space may want avatars to only be spaceships, thinking a pink unicorn might be out of place. A virtual store may not want to allow nude people avatars.

One mechanism for solving this conflict:

  • Users acquire, customize or create one or more avatars, each of whose rendering instructions are actually stored as URL resources on the Internet, like any other 3-D object.
  • Users can submit their avatars to any certifying authority who ensures it complies with whatever appearance rules they have established.
  • A user chooses which avatar they wish to “play” for any specific scene they are in (typically, one of them is their default avatar for any new scene).
  • Whenever a user enters a scene, the scene confirms that the user’s chosen avatar is listed by whatever certifying authority best enforces their rules. If the avatar conforms, that avatar will be shown to other users in that scene. If not, the scene will pick another acceptable avatar (which may be no avatar at all) to render that user to others in the scene (although the user still has the right to see their own chosen avatar within that scene).
